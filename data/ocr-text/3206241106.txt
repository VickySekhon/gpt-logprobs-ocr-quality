|THE ALLEGED MLTRDER AT BRENTFORD.,On Saturday, at the Brentford Petty sessions, Charles Hicks, a shoemaker, 36 years of age, was charged with killing a man named Bernard Burgess, a platelayer on the Great Western railway. Hlicks was lodging with Burgess and his wife, and had been in the house two years. Before he went there Burgess and his wife lived comfortably together, but lately it would appear that Burgess suspected his lodger of undue familiarity with his wife, ;vho was about 10 years younger than himself, but about the same age as the prisoner. The son has stated that his father accused Hicks of taking his motberto the theatre alld keeping her out all night, but when closely questioned on the subject he denied that there was any truth in the report, though his father was quarrelling with Hicks about it on Saturday, the 12th inst. On the 14th the prisoner was seen to follow the deceased man out of the house, go up to him behind, seize hold of him by the collar and shake him. He was heard to say, " I will take care you don't say anything against me. I will murder you if I am hung for it." Deceased did not strike the prisoner or offer any provocotion, but appeared to be trying to get away from Hicks. The latter struck Burgess under the ear with his fist. The blow felled the old man, who remained on the ground, and never stirred a fterwards. The affair took place in the Catherine Wheel-yard, and hearing a disturbance the neighbours went out and attempted to carry Burgess indoors, the prisoner offering some resistance. A surgeon was sent for, and he could not obtain any information as to what had occurred, either from Mrs. Burgess, her daughter, or the prisoner. Burgess died the next day, and a post-mortem examination showed an injury to the brain, which might have been caused by such a blow with the fist as the one given by Hicks. and there was also a small punctured wound, which was probably caused by deceased's head coming in contact with some of the stones in the Catherine Wheel-yard.- Inspector Tarling entered the house shortly after the occurrence, and found the surgeon examining the wounds, and the prisoner, Mrs. B rrgess, and her daughter, in an ad joining room.
